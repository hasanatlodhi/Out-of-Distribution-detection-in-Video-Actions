Introduction

In recent years, action recognition in videos has gained significant attention due to its applications in various fields such as video surveillance, human-computer interaction, and sports analysis. However, a key challenge in action recognition is the ability to detect and classify actions that are unseen during training, known as out-of-distribution (OOD) actions. This blog explores our journey in developing "Actions.ai," a website that utilizes I3D models and novel loss functions to enhance action recognition and OOD detection capabilities.

Understanding the Problem:

Action recognition involves identifying and classifying specific actions or activities within a video. Traditional approaches often rely on handcrafted features and shallow models, which may struggle to capture the rich temporal dynamics and spatial information present in videos. To overcome these limitations, we turned to deep learning techniques, particularly convolutional neural network (CNN) models.

Dataset Selection and Preprocessing:

To train and evaluate our model, we employed the UCF101 dataset, which consists of 101 action categories in a diverse range of videos. This dataset offered a suitable variety of actions to ensure comprehensive training and testing of our system. We performed necessary data preprocessing steps, including video resizing, frame extraction, and temporal sampling, to prepare the data for training.

Video Classification:

Video classification is a challenging task due to the temporal nature of video data. Traditional image classification techniques are insufficient for video classification since they overlook the temporal dependencies between frames. To overcome this limitation, researchers have developed various deep learning architectures that capture temporal information effectively. CNNs, popularly known for their success in image classification, have also been adapted for video classification by incorporating temporal modeling.

Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Detection:

While accurate action recognition is essential, it is equally critical to detect actions that do not belong to the known set of classes. Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection plays a vital role in ensuring the reliability and safety of action recognition systems. OOD actions, also known as unseen actions, are actions that the model has not been trained on. Detecting and flagging these OOD actions helps prevent misclassifications or erroneous predictions.

Using CNN Models

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have revolutionized the field of computer vision. They excel in capturing spatial features from images, making them a valuable asset for video classification. In the context of video processing, CNNs are extended to handle 3D data by considering both spatial and temporal dimensions. These 3D CNNs extract spatio-temporal features from video frames and learn hierarchical representations, enabling accurate action recognition.

Initial Challenges and the Shift to I3D Models:

While CNN models showed promise, we encountered suboptimal results in action recognition and OOD detection. It became evident that the temporal aspect of actions was not being effectively captured. This led us to explore the Inflated 3D ConvNet (I3D), which extends CNNs to process 3D convolutional kernels and learn both spatial and temporal features simultaneously.

Utilizing I3D for Feature Extraction:

To leverage the power of I3D, we incorporated it into our pipeline as a feature extractor. The I3D model is pretrained on large-scale video datasets (e.g., Kinetics) and can be fine-tuned on our specific action recognition task. By extracting features from video frames, we obtained a more robust representation that encapsulates both spatial and temporal characteristics of actions.

Loss Functions for Separating Seen and Unseen Actions:

To tackle the challenge of OOD detection, we explored novel loss functions that encourage the model to distinguish between seen and unseen actions. One approach we adopted was the confidence-based loss function, which assigned higher confidence values to seen actions and lower confidence values to unseen actions. This helped create a clear separation between the two categories, enabling accurate identification and classification.

Results and Performance Evaluation:

The integration of I3D models and novel loss functions yielded significant improvements in action recognition and OOD detection. Through rigorous experimentation and evaluation, we observed higher accuracy in identifying both seen and unseen actions. The use of I3D models allowed for more accurate temporal modeling, leading to improved recognition performance. The confidence-based loss function further enhanced the model's ability to differentiate between seen and unseen actions.
